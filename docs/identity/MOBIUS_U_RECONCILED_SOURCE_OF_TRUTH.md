### Chinese Yi progression

The working semantic progression is:

```text
一  →  意  →  易  →  義
one   intent  change  significance / right relation
```

This chain is an authored mnemonic and motion grammar. It is not a canonical
Chinese etymology. Its strongest use is distributed across states:

- `一` — ground, horizon, cursor, fold axis, or blink;
- `意` — intent gathering in the directed `i`;
- `易` — change concentrated at the `𝒰` hinge;
- `義` — relational significance settling in `us` and outward consequence.

`易` also supplies the productive “change” resonance associated with the
*I Ching* / *Yijing*. The identity may use that fact as a thematic bridge, but
it must not imply that all four characters form one historical lexical chain.

## Reverse-sheet decomposition

Turning the wordmark around exposes another legitimate layer:

```text
MÖBIUS  →  SUIBÖM
```

The opening `SUI` corresponds to Latin *suī*, the reflexive genitive meaning
“of oneself / of itself.” That reading is especially coherent with a system
whose self-reference becomes visible on the inverted sheet and with Korean
`의` occupying genitive/relational territory.

This is a linguistic resonance, not proof that the name was causally selected
by Latin. Diacritics and capitalization must be handled honestly in public
copy: the exact Latin form is *suī*, while the reversed display supplies `SUI`.

The terminal `öM` can become `OM` only through an explicit authored motion:
the literal eyes lift from `ö`, the remaining form normalizes to `o`, and the
pair is re-read in the restored frame. `OM` is therefore a proposed kinetic
Easter egg, not a claim that plain reversal literally spells it. If used, it
must remain secondary to the word's immediate identity and avoid treating a
sacred syllable as decorative brand texture.
